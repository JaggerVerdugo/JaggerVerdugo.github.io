EXOPLANETS



LINDSEY WISER

Lindsey is curious about the formation and evolution of planets. She uses 1D exoplanet atmosphere grid models and retrievals to interpret space-based spectroscopic observations, primarily with JWST and Hubble. Lindsey is also a student shadow with the Pandora SmallSat mission, where she is working on planning telescope commissioning and comms/outreach initiatives.


PETER SMITH

Peter's primary interest is in leveraging the complementarity of spectroscopic data from large, ground-based telescopes and space telescopes like the JWST for inferring the properties of atmospheres of transiting giant planets, such as their climate and chemical composition. They also develop statistical and computational methods for processing and using high spectral resolution data. The broad questions Peter tries to answer include: How do planets form? How do atmospheres transport energy around highly irradiated planets? What can we learn about exoplanets using space telescopes that we cannot from ground telescopes, and vice versa?


JORGE A. SANCHEZ

Jorge works with Jenny Patience and Mike Line at ASU on the characterization of substellar atmospheres, specifically exoplanet and brown dwarf atmospheres. To do this, he uses telescopes on the ground and space to study the light that comes from these objects. Some big questions he seeks to answer are: 1) What are the compositions and climates of the atmospheres of exoplanets and brown dwarfs 2) How do these observables change as a function of effective temperature or gravity in these objects 3) How can we use what we learn from our current data reduction and atmospheric modeling techniques to inform the next generation of exoplanet/brown dwarf science in the coming decade.


SOPHIE R. PHILLIPS

Sophie's primary research is investigating lower drivers of atmospheric dynamics on Earth with the Space Physics group at ASU. More specifically, she investigates seasonal trends and case study characteristics of atmospheric gravity waves (not the big Einstein ones). She previously helped develop the JWST data to exoplanet model comparison widget on this website - go to Utilities to check it out!


YOAV ROTMAN

Yoav's research mainly deals in low resolution spectroscopy of exoplanet atmospheres using space-based observatories including JWST and HST. He is interested in understanding and characterizing the model-data interaction between current models of exoplanet atmospheres and data from these telescopes, and whether their mismatches can inform future work regarding missing physics in our models and instrumentation biases. Yoav's work hopes to answer questions including (a) what physics do we need to include in exoplanet atmospheric models? (b) how can we account for systemic biases from our data? and (c) how much can we trust our inferences of physical processes in exoplanet atmospheres?


LANA TILKE

As a first-year, Lana's research direction is still developing. Generally, they are interested in interrogating the characterization and habitability of terrestrial planets. For their second project, they are working with Professor Joe O'Rourke. In the past, Lana has conducted research on habitability and technosignatures at NASA's Goddard Institute for Space Studies; the University of California, Berkeley; and the SETI Institute.
